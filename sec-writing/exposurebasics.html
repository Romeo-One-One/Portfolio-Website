Exposure Controls

Shutter Speed, Aperture, and ISO

Posted by Remy Dinh on December 7th, 2015

In digital photography, there are three basic exposure settings.

What is an Exposure?

An exposure is an image that has been captured by a camera while following the instructions of certain controls.

All of these controls affect the amount of light that reaches the camera sensor during exposure. This serves to control the brightness of the image, but each control has its own limits and trade-offs.

Shutter Speed (Exposure Time)

Shutter Speed controls how long the camera shutter will open, allowing light from the scene to hit the sensor. The longer the shutter is open, more light will hit the sensor, resulting in a brighter exposure.

Even though this is what most photographers call it, "shutter speed" is an inaccurate name because it is always indicated as a time value such as seconds or fractions of a second.

Shutter speed is always denoted as an amount of time, such as "1/60 sec" or "1/2000 sec". Shutter speed values can also be greater than a whole second, such as 2 seconds or 30 seconds.

Aperture

The Aperture setting controls how wide the lens diaphragm opens up, letting in more or less light depending on the size of the opening.

Every lens has a set of curved blades that open and close to change the size of the lens diaphragm.

ISO (Signal Gain)

ISO controls how much the signal captured by the camera will get amplified after capture. What does this mean?

When you want to listen to a recording of someone's voice, what do you do when you can barely hear at the current volume level?

The simplest solution is often to just crank up the volume, especially if you don't have the time to go back and re-record the person's voice. However, this comes with the side effect of also increasing the volume of the surrounding background noise. With audio, this background noise is typically distinguished as a random, faint hissing as a result of the surrounding atmosphere and interference from other devices that generate signals.
